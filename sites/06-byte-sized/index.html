The cost of a lock you never contend

Everyone knows uncontended mutexes are "basically free." Then you profile a hot loop that takes a lock 40 million times a second and watch it fall off a cliff. Here's what the CPU is actually doing on that fast path — atomics, fences, and the cache line you didn't know you were sharing:

#rust #performance #concurrency
counter.rs 
// Mutex: correct, but the CAS + fence isn't free
fn bump(c: &Mutex<u64>) {
    let mut g = c.lock().unwrap();
    *g += 1;
}

// Relaxed atomic: ~7x faster, no lock
fn bump_atomic(c: &AtomicU64) {
    c.fetch_add(1, Ordering::Relaxed);
}

// but share a cache line and it's
// slower than the Mutex. measure it.

About

say hello →

I've spent a decade taking systems that work on one machine and making them keep working on a thousand — which mostly means learning, over and over, that the network is not your friend.

By day I work on the storage engine at Halyard Data, where we build a column store for teams that outgrew their warehouse but aren't ready to run their own. Before that I was at a payments company chasing tail latency, and before that I wrote firmware for industrial sensors — which is where I learned that "it compiles" and "it ships" are separated by an ocean of the physical world.

This blog is where I think out loud. I'm suspicious of frameworks that hide the cost of what they do, fond of code you can hold entirely in your head, and convinced that most performance work is really just deleting things. If any of that resonates, we'll get along.

When I'm not staring at flame graphs I'm restoring a very stubborn 1987 road bike, learning just enough cello to annoy the neighbours, and reading more distributed systems papers than is strictly healthy.
